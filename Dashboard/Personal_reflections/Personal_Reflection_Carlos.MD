## Executive Summary

Phase 2 pushed me into territory I had never explored before: translating a technical simulation into a visual narrative designed to persuade real people. While Phase 1 was fundamentally an engineering challenge, Phase 2 was something different. It required me to think simultaneously as an engineer, a communicator, and someone who understands what it takes to convince a person to make a financial decision. That combination turned out to be one of the most valuable things I brought to this project.

---

## My Role in Phase 2

My responsibilities in this phase centered on the simulation upgrade, the data processor, and the majority of the dashboard implementation. After Andrés established the initial dashboard structure and visual identity, I took over the connection of the real simulation data to the frontend, defined the full narrative structure, and built out most of the individual chart sections. The work was iterative and often nonlinear: sections were reorganized, charts were moved between acts, and copy was rewritten multiple times until the story felt right.

Martín covered testing and optimization. The division of labor felt natural and complementary, largely because we had already worked through the friction of collaboration in Phase 1.

---

## What Marked Me Most About This Phase

The process of building the dashboard narrative was unlike anything I had done before in an engineering context. Going from a broad idea, "solar energy is good, poor planning is bad," to a specific four-act story, and then grounding every step of that story in charts that were not just accurate but visually impactful, was a new kind of challenge entirely. I had never thought carefully about what a person feels when they see a gauge chart versus a scatter plot, or why withholding certain data until the right moment in a presentation makes it land harder. Working through those decisions, one section at a time, was genuinely fascinating and opened up a way of thinking about engineering deliverables that I want to carry forward.

---

## The Moment Everything Clicked

There was a specific moment when I saw the completed dashboard for the first time as a whole, after all the sections were connected and the animations were running, where everything we had discussed as a team finally made sense as a single coherent thing. Seeing Andrés' original design, which had seemed abstract when he first showed it to us, fully realized with real simulation data, real narrative copy, and charts that told the story we had planned, was genuinely satisfying in a way that finishing a coding task rarely is. It was the first time I experienced what it feels like to deliver something that is not just correct but intentional.

---

## What I Learned About Myself

This phase made me more aware of something I had not fully articulated before: the combination of a finance background and an engineering education gives me a different way of framing problems than most of my peers. Engineers tend to ask "does this work?" I tend to also ask "does this create value for whoever is paying for it, and can I prove it with data?" That orientation shaped the entire narrative strategy of the dashboard, from the decision to lead with financial pain in The Problem section, to the deliberate withholding of cost and CO2 data until The Solution, to the closing equivalency cards that translate abstract numbers into something a non-technical person can feel.

What made this particularly effective in the team context is that my teammates, coming from an animation and graphic design background, complemented this approach perfectly. Where I brought a financial and strategic lens, they brought an eye for visual impact and design quality that I simply do not have. The result was a dashboard that was both strategically sound and visually polished, and I do not think either dimension would have been as strong without the other.

---

## Looking Forward

Phase 2 confirmed something I want to keep developing: the ability to design technical tools with a clear answer to the question of who benefits from them and how. As we move into Phase 3 and begin thinking about machine learning integration, I want to bring the same orientation. A model that optimizes energy configuration without accounting for installation costs and financial return is not a product, it is an academic exercise. Making it useful means asking the same questions we asked when building this dashboard: who is this for, what decision does it support, and what does the data need to say to make that decision obvious.

---

## Conclusion

Phase 2 was the most creatively demanding part of this project so far. It required me to move between technical implementation, narrative design, and financial reasoning in a way that felt genuinely interdisciplinary. Seeing the finished dashboard, knowing every element had a reason for being there and a place in the story, was the most satisfying deliverable I have produced in this program. I am proud of what the team built, and more importantly, I understand why it works.
